ABRAHAM LINCOLN
Abraham Lincoln rose to the presidency in 1860, sparking the withdrawal of seven Southern states that formed the Confederacy followed by four more once fighting broke out between North and South. The nation was soon consumed by a bloody civil war as Lincoln pledged to preserve the Union, uphold federal law, and stop secession. The conflict dragged on for over four years, claiming more than 600,000 lives. During its course, Lincoln issued the Emancipation Proclamation, declaring freedom for enslaved people within the Confederacy. This shifted the purpose of the war from merely saving the Union to a struggle for human liberty. As the first Republican president, he oversaw the Union’s triumph, settling the debate over state sovereignty versus federal authority. Tragically, he was assassinated just days after Confederate forces surrendered, leaving behind a legacy as one of America’s most admired leaders. Born in 1809 in a humble log cabin in Kentucky, Lincoln grew up poor and largely self-taught, developing a passion for books, humor, and hard work. He briefly served in the Black Hawk War, studied law independently, and later entered politics as a Whig in the Illinois legislature. By 1847, he held a seat in the U.S. House of Representatives, where he opposed the Mexican-American War. In the 1850s, he joined the new Republican Party, and though he lost the 1858 Senate race to Stephen Douglas, his eloquence in the debates gained him national recognition and helped secure the 1860 Republican presidential nomination.the preservation of the Union, safeguarding of democracy, and destruction of slavery—secured his place in history as a leader who sought “a more perfect union” with compassion and resolve
